
Cybersecurity Policy Development Project
📌 Project Overview
As a Cybersecurity Analyst, I developed this comprehensive policy framework to establish a robust governance structure for a dummy enterprise called LukmanBtech. This serves as a centralized documentation hub for internal security standards, ensuring that technical controls are backed by enforceable administrative mandates.
The goal of this project is to bridge the gap between technical implementation and business compliance by defining clear expectations for user behavior, system configurations, and data privacy.
💼 Business Purpose
Modern organizations face a landscape of evolving threats and strict regulatory requirements. The purpose of this documentation is to:
Mitigate Risk: Reduce the likelihood of data breaches caused by human error or weak authentication.
Standardize Operations: Provide a uniform standard for IT and Security teams.
Establish Accountability: Define the roles and responsibilities of employees and third-party contractors.
🏛️ Standards & Frameworks Applied
To ensure industry-leading quality, these policies were mapped against the following frameworks:
NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF): Specifically the Identify, Protect, and Govern functions.
ISO/IEC 27001: Alignment with Annex A controls regarding access and cryptography.
⚖️ Legal & Regulatory Alignment
The policies are designed to help an organization meet the requirements of:
GDPR / CCPA: Ensuring data subject privacy and "Privacy by Design."
HIPAA / PCI-DSS: Specifically regarding the protection of sensitive PII/PHI and financial data.
📄 Policies Developed
1. Password Construction Standard
Defines complexity requirements, rotation cycles, and MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication) mandates.
Key Focus: Moving toward NIST 800-63B standards (prioritizing length over frequent rotation).
2. Acceptable Use Standard (AUP)
Outlines the permitted and prohibited uses of company-owned assets (hardware, software, and network).
Key Focus: Preventing shadow IT and protecting intellectual property.
3. Access Management Policy
Establishes the lifecycle of user access, including provisioning and periodic access reviews.
Key Focus: The Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP).
4. Identity Management Policy (IDM)
Governs the creation, maintenance, and decommissioning of digital identities.
Key Focus: Single Sign-On (SSO) integration and Directory Service hygiene.
5. Privacy Management Policy
Sets the standard for how the organization collects, stores, and shares personal data.
Key Focus: Data minimization and transparency.
🚀 Why These Policies Matter
Policy is the foundation of the security stack. Without a written policy:
Technical controls lack authority: You cannot discipline an employee for bypassing a control if the "rule" was never documented.
Audits become impossible: Auditors verify compliance against your stated policies. No policy equals to an automatic failure.
Incident Response is delayed: Policy defines who has the authority to make decisions during a crisis
📈 Business Impact
Reduction in Cyber Insurance Premiums: Demonstrating a formal policy framework is often a prerequisite for lower insurance rates.
Enhanced Brand Trust: Customers are more likely to share data with an organization that displays transparent privacy and security standards.
Operational Efficiency: Automated on-boarding and off-boarding (via IDM/Access policies) reduce IT overhead.
🛠️ Use Cases
New Hire On-boarding: Using the AUP to set expectations during the first week of employment.
Internal Audit: Using the Access Management Policy as a checklist for quarterly user-access reviews.
Security Architecture Planning: Using the Password Construction Policy to configure IAM settings in AWS/Azure.
